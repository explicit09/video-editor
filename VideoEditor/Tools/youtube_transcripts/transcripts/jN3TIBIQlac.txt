Now, technically, B-roll is defined as
any secondary footage, shots of people,
places, or objects that support or
illustrate the story. You see it
everywhere. Documentaries, promos,
YouTube videos, branded content, social
media. It's as common as dialogue. But
while most editors treat it like

Here's something almost no one outside
of professional cutting rooms talks
about. There are two completely
different species of B-roll. Number one,
sequential B-roll. This is a chain of
shots that shows a process, a journey,
or a flow of progressive action. Think
of someone making a cake, flour, hitting
the bowl, cracking the egg, whisking,
pouring in the mix, putting into the
oven, and then serving it. Each shot is
a link in a chain. Together, they form a
mini sequence that progresses over time.
This is not just beautiful imagery. It's
story logic in visual form. A camera
operator takes an event and breaks it
down into different shots with different
shot sizes and camera movement. There
could be five shots in the mini
sequence, or there could be over 500.
And number two, illustrative B-roll.
These are standalone images. A beautiful
city skyline at dawn. A building showing
the location of the upcoming action. A
closeup of a ticking clock. A flower pot
in the corner of a room. They're not
part of a chain. They are individual
shots that illustrate individual objects
or places. You can't edit both types the
same way. And if you've never
consciously separated them before,
that's your first breakthrough. Now,
