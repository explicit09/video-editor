# Color

## Rules

### Rule: Story-Driven Approach First
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Begin with tone and story of the piece, not just aesthetic preference. Ask "What is the tone? What's the idea behind it?" before making any color decisions.
- **Reasoning:** Color grading should serve narrative and emotional intent. Starting with story context ensures decisions reinforce the piece's message rather than impose arbitrary looks.

### Rule: Balance Before Looks
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Always establish overall balance first, then layer looks on top. Get overall picture balanced → try look strips → apply globally → refine per-shot.
- **Reasoning:** A balanced foundation prevents technical issues and makes stylistic adjustments more effective. Looks applied to unbalanced footage often look unmotivated or create unexpected color casts.

### Rule: Color Management Foundation
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Proper input/output color spaces are a prerequisite for confident grading. Working in managed color space prevents clipping and color shifts.
- **Reasoning:** Without proper color management, you're working blind. Managed color spaces ensure predictable results, prevent data loss in highlights/shadows, and guarantee your grade translates accurately to deliverables.

### Rule: Avoid Aggressive Saturation Pushes
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Cranking saturation globally creates dingy, yellow skin tones. Use selective color to boost warmth in specific tones instead.
- **Reasoning:** Global saturation boosts affect all colors equally, distorting skin tones and creating unnatural, muddy results. Selective saturation maintains believability while enhancing specific color ranges.

### Rule: Warm Tones Without Dingy Look
- **Applies to:** Weddings, portraits, interview-based content
- **Rule:** Work through shadows and selectively boost warmth rather than pushing the entire image warm. Use anchor points to keep teeth and dress neutral while warming skin tones.
- **Reasoning:** Strategic warmth in shadows adds flattering dimension to skin without affecting other elements. Anchor points preserve color integrity in critical areas while creating cohesion.

### Rule: Keep Processes Sustainable for Volume
- **Applies to:** High-volume workflows, event coverage
- **Rule:** When handling hundreds of shots, simplify node trees. If you can't paste and refine a look across hundreds of shots quickly, it's too complex.
- **Reasoning:** Overly complex grading workflows become bottlenecks. Streamlined processes allow consistent quality at scale and reduce fatigue and error in repetitive work.

### Rule: Bleach Bypass for Contrast Without Saturation
- **Applies to:** Stylistic grading, dramatic narratives
- **Rule:** Bleach bypass increases contrast while decreasing saturation, creating a snappy, bold look. Famous examples: Saving Private Ryan, Seven.
- **Reasoning:** Bleach bypass isolates luminance contrast from color, delivering visual punch without oversaturated colors. It's effective for high-stakes, dramatic material.

### Rule: Texture and Spatial Composition in Grading
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Use vignettes, power windows, and spatial adjustments to control viewer attention. The DP does their job on set; the colorist continues that work.
- **Reasoning:** Spatial grading reshapes visual hierarchy and guides eye movement. It's the difference between flat color correction and intentional, cinematic color composition.

### Rule: 80% of Color Work Happens in Camera
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** You cannot fix bad lighting in post. Proper exposure, lighting design, and camera settings are the foundation for effective grading.
- **Reasoning:** Post-production color grading works best when the source material is well-exposed and properly lit. Fighting bad source material is exhausting and limits quality potential.

### Rule: Shot Matching Is 90% of Grading
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Shot matching is the foundation for everything else in a grade. Perfect shot-to-shot consistency must come first.
- **Reasoning:** Viewers are distracted by inconsistency. Solid shot matching creates visual continuity and makes stylistic choices read as intentional rather than accidental.

### Rule: Use Vectorscope and Waveform Data
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Use vectorscope and waveform monitors to see exactly where colors sit. Never rely on monitor appearance alone.
- **Reasoning:** Monitors can deceive due to ambient light, calibration drift, and human perception. Scopes provide objective data, prevent clipping, and ensure technical accuracy.

### Rule: Different Cameras Have Different Color Science
- **Applies to:** Multi-camera shoots, mixed formats
- **Rule:** Canon Log, Sony S-Log, and Blackmagic Film shoot differently. Match skin tones first, then match overall image tone.
- **Reasoning:** Each camera's log curve behaves uniquely. Starting with skin tone matching as a reference point ensures perceptual consistency across different camera sources.

### Rule: Color Management: Elevator vs. Fire Escape
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Color management is "an elevator approach"; manual grading is "climbing the fire escape." Both get you there, but one is faster.
- **Reasoning:** Leveraging LUTs, color spaces, and automation accelerates workflows. Manual node-by-node work is valuable for refinement but shouldn't replace efficient foundational tools.

### Rule: Dodge and Burn With Parallel Nodes
- **Applies to:** Advanced grading, character-driven work
- **Rule:** Use power windows to lift or darken specific areas, reshaping how light falls. Parallel nodes don't accumulate transformations.
- **Reasoning:** Parallel node architecture prevents compounding color shifts and maintains clean, predictable adjustments. Isolation prevents unintended color bleeding into adjacent areas.

### Rule: Isolate Faces, Then Add Warmth Strategically
- **Applies to:** Portraits, interviews, dialogue-heavy content
- **Rule:** Isolate faces, lift exposure, and add red/magenta in shadows for warmth. Keep highlights and midtones controlled.
- **Reasoning:** Face isolation ensures subject emphasis. Strategic shadow warmth flatters skin while maintaining highlight believability and prevents blown-out, plastic appearance.

### Rule: Undertones Matter—Control Black Levels With Curves
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Control black levels with curves to add richness; don't let blacks go to pure black without character.
- **Reasoning:** Blacks with subtle color undertones (slight blue, green, or warm cast) feel more cinematic and intentional than neutral black. This adds depth and sophistication to the overall image.

### Rule: Warm Highlights Plus Cool Shadows Creates Depth
- **Applies to:** Narrative, cinematic work
- **Rule:** Create depth through complementary temperature shifts: warm (amber/orange) highlights paired with cool (blue) shadows.
- **Reasoning:** This temperature contrast is inherently cinematic, mimics natural light behavior, and creates visual separation and dimensionality between light and shadow.

### Rule: Use Custom Curves to Control Tonal Range
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Pull shadows down for moodiness and manage highlights for believability. Use curves to shape the exact tonal compression you need.
- **Reasoning:** Curves provide granular control over how the image compresses. Custom curves prevent one-size-fits-all approaches and allow tone sculpting that serves the content's mood.

### Rule: LUTs Are Lookup Tables—Choose Intentionally
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Choose LUTs made for your specific camera, not generic ones. Understand what they do before applying.
- **Reasoning:** Camera-specific LUTs account for that camera's unique color science. Generic LUTs can introduce unexpected color shifts and look unprofessional on mismatched cameras.

### Rule: Quick First Look, Then Loop and Refine
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Apply initial look to ALL shots quickly → loop back → refine per-shot. Don't get stuck perfecting a single shot.
- **Reasoning:** Applying a rough look globally first shows how it plays across the entire piece. This prevents over-refinement of isolated shots and ensures the overall grade feels cohesive.

### Rule: Prioritize Ruthlessly in Time-Constrained Sessions
- **Applies to:** Commercial work, tight deadlines
- **Rule:** Quickly identify what matters most. Red dress color matters more than actor's skin tone, which matters more than blemish removal.
- **Reasoning:** Knowing which elements drive the piece's visual impact lets you allocate time strategically. Perfecting low-priority details at the expense of primary elements is inefficient.

### Rule: Communicate Throughout the Process
- **Applies to:** Client-facing sessions, collaborative work
- **Rule:** Narrate what you're doing as you work. Silent changes confuse clients and erode confidence.
- **Reasoning:** Explaining your reasoning builds trust and ensures client expectations align with your creative direction. Silent work feels like a black box and invites second-guessing.

### Rule: Use Subtractive Saturation for Control
- **Applies to:** Stylistic grading, desaturated looks
- **Rule:** Desaturate globally, then selectively boost specific colors rather than saturating globally from the start.
- **Reasoning:** Subtractive approaches prevent oversaturation and color shifts. Desaturating first creates a neutral canvas; boosting specific colors then feels intentional and controlled.

### Rule: Film Grain and Organic Details Must Blend Naturally
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Film grain and texture must match the source's natural characteristics. Pixelated or glowing artifacts break immersion.
- **Reasoning:** Artificial grain that doesn't blend breaks the illusion. Grain should feel organic to the camera and lighting, not painted on. Mismatched grain destroys realism.

## Tips

1. **Scout your monitor before grading.** Spend 5 minutes at the start of each session checking monitor calibration, ambient light, and reference materials. A well-calibrated environment prevents costly mistakes later.

2. **Create a shot matching template early.** Identify one representative shot (typically an establishing shot or close-up with good lighting) and use it as your reference. Grade this shot first, then paste and refine the look across all others. This dramatically speeds up matching.

3. **Organize your nodes logically.** Label nodes by function (e.g., "PRIMARY BALANCE," "SKIN TONE WARMTH," "SHADOWS LIFT," "VIGNETTE"). Clear organization prevents confusion, makes collaboration easier, and lets you quickly locate adjustments for revisions.

4. **Use power windows more than saturation curves.** Most color issues are spatial, not global. Before reaching for saturation adjustments, try isolating the problem area with a power window. You'll achieve cleaner, more intentional results.

5. **Save looks as presets only after testing on multiple shots.** A look that works beautifully on one shot may fail on another. Always test on 3-5 representative shots (different lighting, skin tones, clothing colors) before saving a look as a reusable preset.

6. **Reference color palette from the art department or production design.** If available, request color swatches or production stills showing intended color themes. Grading should amplify the production's existing color language, not contradict it.

7. **Avoid grading in darkness.** Dark rooms can lead to crushing shadows and blown highlights. Grade in a dimly lit room with neutral walls. Your eyes will stay fresh longer and your grades will translate better to different viewing environments.

8. **Check your grade on multiple displays if possible.** Your primary monitor might be calibrated, but your grade will be viewed on TVs, phones, and laptops. Quickly reference on secondary displays to catch unexpected color shifts.

9. **Use fade-outs and fade-ins to transition between looks.** When a scene requires a color shift (e.g., entering candlelit room), use a short fade rather than a hard cut. This feels more cinematic and less jarring.

10. **Keep a "before and after" thumbnail of your primary shot.** Side-by-side comparison of your starting point vs. final grade helps you avoid over-grading and lets you communicate change magnitude to clients clearly.
