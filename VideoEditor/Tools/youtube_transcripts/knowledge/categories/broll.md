# B-Roll Knowledge

## Rules

### Rule: Energy Match Between B-Roll and Cut
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** B-roll energy must match the cut—pacing and energy of supplemental footage should sync with primary edit rhythm. Slow B-roll in a fast-paced edit (or vice versa) creates a jarring feeling.
- **Reasoning:** Visual pacing and audio pacing must align. Mismatched energy disorients viewers and breaks immersion, regardless of how good the footage is individually.

### Rule: Match Footage Quality and Color
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** When using stock footage or mixed sources, grade and color-correct them to match primary footage. Don't leave color differences unaddressed.
- **Reasoning:** Mismatched color immediately signals "stock footage inserted here." Color consistency maintains the illusion of seamless storytelling and professional execution.

### Rule: Precision Over Length
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Use only what's needed—trim B-roll to exact length required for pacing. Longer B-roll doesn't equal better; precision pacing matters more.
- **Reasoning:** Extended B-roll shots feel like padding and test viewer patience. Tight, well-trimmed shots maintain momentum and hold attention.

### Rule: B-Roll Supports Audio Narrative
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** B-roll shows what's being discussed in voiceover or primary dialogue. It illustrates, clarifies, and reinforces the audio message.
- **Reasoning:** Audio and visual must work together. B-roll that illustrates what viewers hear creates comprehension and emotional resonance.

### Rule: Visual Interest and Context
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** B-roll provides visual interest to break monotony (talking head/interviews) and establishes location and context (all content types). Use it intentionally for these purposes.
- **Reasoning:** Extended talking heads fatigue viewers. Strategic B-roll cuts keep attention and provide visual proof of claims made in dialogue or voiceover.

### Rule: Rhythm Determines Cut Approach
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Fast cuts for high-energy moments; slow reveals for contemplative moments. Match the visual pacing to emotional tone and message.
- **Reasoning:** Cut pace is a storytelling tool. Fast cuts signal urgency and excitement; slow cuts signal reflection and gravitas. Using the wrong pacing for the moment undermines the message.

### Rule: Energy Matching Requires Intentional Selection
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Don't just throw B-roll over voiceover randomly—match the visual energy to the audio energy. Select shots that reinforce, not contradict, the tone.
- **Reasoning:** Arbitrary B-roll placement feels amateurish. Intentional selection shows the story was carefully considered and crafted.

### Rule: Pre-Trim B-Roll in Selects Timeline
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Pre-trim B-roll in a "Selects" timeline to only the usable portions. This reduces decision-making and speeds up main edit assembly.
- **Reasoning:** A curated selection of pre-trimmed clips accelerates editing by eliminating irrelevant footage and frame options during the main edit phase.

### Rule: Archive Footage Requires Context
- **Applies to:** Documentary and historical content
- **Rule:** Archive footage needs visual context to establish it as old/reference material. Use effects, filters, or title cards to signal temporal difference.
- **Reasoning:** Without context, viewers may misunderstand the relevance or timeline of archive footage. Clear signaling maintains narrative coherence.

### Rule: B-Roll Should Support, Not Compete
- **Applies to:** Talking-head and interview content
- **Rule:** For talking-head voiceovers, B-roll should support and illustrate what's being said, not compete for attention. Keep B-roll compositionally simpler than primary footage.
- **Reasoning:** In interview-driven content, the speaker's face and words are primary. B-roll that's too visually complex diverts attention and creates cognitive conflict.

## Tips

1. **Create a B-roll subclip library** - Make a dedicated bin of pre-trimmed B-roll subclips organized by category (establishing shots, details, motion, etc.). Reference this bin during editing for faster selection.

2. **Stagger B-roll timing** - Avoid cutting B-roll exactly on beat with every dialogue or voiceover line. Offset some B-roll cuts slightly earlier or later to create a natural, less mechanical rhythm.

3. **Use motion as a visual punctuation** - Match B-roll cuts to motion peaks: a pan finishing, a subject moving into frame, or a gesture completing. This creates imperceptible cuts that feel smooth.

4. **Grade archive footage differently** - Apply desaturation, grain, or a subtle color shift to archive footage to visually distinguish it from primary footage without being heavy-handed. This signals temporal difference immediately.

5. **Test B-roll with sound off** - Watch your edit with audio muted to check that B-roll pacing and energy work visually independent of dialogue. If the rhythm feels off without sound, adjust timing before adding audio refinements.
