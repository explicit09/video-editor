# Cuts

## Rules

### Rule: Cut on Action
- **Applies to:** All visual content
- **Rule:** Time cuts to occur during visible movement rather than during static moments. Cuts become visually less apparent when the audience is watching movement on screen.
- **Reasoning:** Movement naturally draws attention and masks the transition point. Viewers' eyes are tracking the motion, making the cut feel seamless rather than jarring. This is one of the most fundamental techniques in invisible editing.

### Rule: Cut on Energy Shifts
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Use natural energy transitions and pauses as cut points. When the energy level of a scene changes—volume, pacing, emotional intensity—that moment signals an appropriate cutting opportunity.
- **Reasoning:** Viewers unconsciously perceive energy changes as natural pause points. Cutting at these moments feels motivated by the content itself rather than arbitrary, creating organic flow through the piece.

### Rule: Never Cut Mid-Sentence
- **Applies to:** All spoken content
- **Rule:** Maintain complete thoughts and sentences when making cuts. Avoid cutting during someone's speech in a way that fragments their words or message.
- **Reasoning:** Interrupting speech breaks the communicative intention and distracts the viewer. Complete sentences preserve clarity and professionalism while maintaining the speaker's rhythm and credibility.

### Rule: J-Cut and L-Cut for Smooth Transitions
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Overlap audio from the next clip while showing the previous video (J-cut), or overlap video from the next clip while continuing previous audio (L-cut). This creates bridge transitions between clips.
- **Reasoning:** These overlapping transitions provide audio-visual continuity that makes cuts feel less abrupt. They allow the viewer to anticipate what's coming while maintaining engagement with the outgoing shot.

### Rule: Trim Dead Space at Clip Beginnings
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Remove silence and introductory dead space from the front end of clips, cutting them to begin exactly when the meaningful content starts.
- **Reasoning:** Dead space slows pacing and feels unprofessional. Tight trimming maintains momentum and respects the viewer's attention. It improves overall editing polish and keeps the piece moving forward.

### Rule: Every Shot Must Serve Purpose
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Ensure every shot included in the final edit connects to the narrative or serves a specific communicative function. Eliminate shots that don't advance the story, provide necessary context, or enhance understanding.
- **Reasoning:** Purposeless shots create confusion and dilute impact. Each shot is an opportunity to communicate something to the audience. Strategic inclusion keeps the narrative clear and maintains viewer engagement.

### Rule: Cutting Decisions Are Creative Choices
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Recognize that what you cut out is just as important and creative as what you keep. Cutting is not merely removal—it's a fundamental creative decision that shapes meaning, pacing, and emotional impact.
- **Reasoning:** The removed footage influences tone and message as much as the included footage. Thoughtful cutting choices determine whether a piece feels rushed, contemplative, powerful, or slow. Embrace cutting as a primary creative tool.

### Rule: Maintain Contextual Pacing
- **Applies to:** Context-dependent (interviews, montages, conversations)
- **Rule:** Cut with consideration for content type: maintain breathing room and natural pacing for interviews and conversations; cut tightly and dynamically for montages and action sequences.
- **Reasoning:** Different content types require different temporal relationships. Interviews benefit from space for contemplation and response; montages gain energy from rapid, tight cutting. Matching cutting pace to content creates appropriate emotional resonance.

### Rule: Cut Approximately 20% of Recorded Time
- **Applies to:** Podcast and interview content
- **Rule:** Plan to reduce raw recorded material to approximately 80% of its original length. This typically removes dead time, false starts, and repetition while maintaining substance.
- **Reasoning:** Raw recordings contain natural pauses, technical issues, and redundant material that slows pacing. Removing roughly 20% of recorded time creates a tighter, more professional final product while preserving all essential content.

### Rule: Switch Cut Technique for Multi-Camera Footage
- **Applies to:** Podcasts, interviews, multi-camera setups
- **Rule:** While monitoring playback of multi-camera footage, use keyboard shortcuts to switch between camera angles in real-time, recording your editorial decisions. This creates dynamic angle variety and improves pacing without needing separate editing passes.
- **Reasoning:** Real-time switching during playback allows you to make cutting decisions instinctively while experiencing the material's natural rhythm. This workflow is faster and often produces more engaging angle selections than post-editing.

### Rule: Organize Footage When Overwhelmed
- **Applies to:** All content with large volumes of footage
- **Rule:** When you have excessive footage, sync all material and use color labels to track which clips are overused versus underutilized. This visual system reveals patterns in your editorial decisions and prevents over-reliance on certain shots.
- **Reasoning:** Large amounts of footage can lead to repetitive cutting choices based on convenience rather than intention. Visual organization provides clarity and helps you make more diverse, purposeful editorial decisions across the entire piece.

### Rule: Problem-Solve Rather Than Over-Decorate
- **Applies to:** All content types
- **Rule:** Prioritize fixing poorly filmed or recorded material through smart editing choices over adding flashy techniques. Sometimes editing is about making the best of what exists rather than showcasing editing skills.
- **Reasoning:** Audiences respond to clear communication and good storytelling before they notice editing technique. Solving practical problems—poor audio, weak performances, inadequate coverage—creates a solid foundation. Fancy cuts on weak content distract rather than enhance.

## Tips

- **Batch similar shots together:** When you have insufficient footage for the cut, strategically use montages with tight cutting to cover large time spans or show sequences efficiently. Use archive footage sparingly, as it can feel slower than original material.

- **Seek feedback on uncertain cuts:** If a cut doesn't feel quite right, share it with others for fresh perspective. What feels obvious to you after hours of editing may read differently to a viewer encountering it for the first time.

- **Stay flexible with scripted content:** While scripts provide structure, don't follow them so rigidly that you miss better pacing and flow. Sometimes a deviation creates more natural, engaging rhythm than strict adherence.

- **Sync and compare:** After assembling rough cuts, pay attention to which footage you gravitate toward. Your intuitive reaching for certain shots often indicates what works best; analyze why those shots are winning.

- **Cut between cuts:** If a single cut isn't working, sometimes the solution isn't to improve that one transition but to reconsider what comes before and after it. Context changes everything in editing.

- **Build in margins:** Leave slightly more material than you need at clip edges. This gives you flexibility during fine-tuning and prevents the stress of having cut too aggressively.
