# Hooks

## Rules

### Rule: First-Second Grab
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Opening seconds are critical—establish whether a viewer stays or leaves. Must grab attention immediately.
- **Reasoning:** Viewer attention is finite and front-loaded. The first impression determines whether the audience invests time in your content. A weak opening loses viewers before they see anything of substance.

### Rule: Trailer-Style Intro
- **Applies to:** Podcasts, YouTube videos
- **Rule:** Create a short intro (30 seconds max) that pulls the best hooks and most compelling moments from the full content.
- **Reasoning:** A curated highlight reel primes the audience for what's coming and creates anticipation. It's easier to maintain interest when viewers know a payoff is waiting.

### Rule: Extended Intro for Multiple Moments
- **Applies to:** Podcasts, longer-form content
- **Rule:** If there are multiple strong hooks available, you can extend the intro slightly beyond 30 seconds to showcase them.
- **Reasoning:** Variety in the opening sequence maintains momentum. Multiple hook points give different viewers different reasons to stay invested.

### Rule: Hook Plus Context
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** A good intro doesn't just hook the audience—it sets up expectations for what the viewer will get from the full piece.
- **Reasoning:** Context transforms curiosity into commitment. Viewers are more likely to stay when they understand the promised value or journey ahead.

### Rule: Authority and Intrigue
- **Applies to:** Educational, informational content
- **Rule:** Opening statements should establish authority and intrigue simultaneously.
- **Reasoning:** Audiences need to believe you know what you're talking about AND want to hear more. This combination builds credibility while maintaining engagement.

### Rule: Memory Context in Archives
- **Applies to:** Documentary, story-driven content
- **Rule:** If using archive footage in the intro, establish it as a memory upfront so viewers understand the temporal context.
- **Reasoning:** Archive material can confuse viewers about timeline. Clear labeling prevents cognitive friction and allows the audience to focus on emotional impact.

### Rule: Visual or Verbal Punch
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Start with something visually or verbally compelling—don't ease into it.
- **Reasoning:** Gradual build-ups feel slow. A strong opening moment (striking imagery, provocative statement, dramatic action) triggers engagement immediately.

### Rule: Text and Animation Highlights
- **Applies to:** YouTube, short-form video
- **Rule:** Use text overlays and animations to highlight key quotes or questions in the opening.
- **Reasoning:** On-screen text reinforces audio and accommodates silent viewers. Strategic highlighting draws the eye to the most important moment.

### Rule: Rapid-Fire Shots
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Quick cuts (0.5–2 second shots) maintain attention in openers.
- **Reasoning:** Fast-paced editing triggers the brain's novelty-response systems. Rapid cuts feel energetic and prevent passive viewing.

### Rule: Energy Alignment
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Match the visual energy to the content promise in the hook.
- **Reasoning:** Misalignment creates cognitive dissonance. If your opening feels chaotic but your content is meditative (or vice versa), the audience feels misled.

### Rule: Three-Second Mandate
- **Applies to:** Short-form video (reels, TikTok, shorts)
- **Rule:** Hook in the first 3 seconds is mandatory for short-form content.
- **Reasoning:** Short-form platform algorithms and viewer behavior both prioritize immediate engagement. Missing the first 3 seconds means algorithmic invisibility and high drop-off rates.

### Rule: Visible Transitions for Hooks
- **Applies to:** Short-form video (reels, shorts, TikTok)
- **Rule:** Use visible transitions (wipeLeft, wipeRight) for hooks, not invisible ones (crossDissolve).
- **Reasoning:** Visible transitions signal to the viewer that the hook is distinct from the main content. This creates a mental break that helps the audience reset attention for the body.

## Tips

- **Lead with your best moment.** Don't save the most compelling content for later. Front-load it, then contextualize it.
- **Test your opening on someone unfamiliar with the topic.** If they look away before the hook lands, trim or strengthen the opening.
- **Use the cold-open technique:** Start with the most compelling moment, then backtrack to explain context. This is more effective than building toward the payoff.
- **Remember the silent viewer.** 80%+ of your audience watches without sound on social platforms. Captions and strong visuals are mandatory for hooks to land.
- **Avoid apologizing or over-explaining in your opening.** Jump straight into the content. Phrases like "So here's the thing..." or "Let me start by saying..." waste precious seconds.
