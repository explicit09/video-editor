# Transitions

## Rules

### Rule: Motivated Transitions
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Transitions should serve a purpose, not just be visually interesting. Valid reasons include: cutting on action, matching energy, supporting emotional beats, or creating rhythm.
- **Reasoning:** Every transition should earn its place. Unmotivated transitions break immersion and signal amateurism. Purpose-driven transitions feel intentional and professional.

### Rule: No Visual Novelty Without Narrative
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Avoid transitions that exist purely for visual interest without narrative or emotional purpose.
- **Reasoning:** Flashy transitions distract from content. They draw attention to themselves rather than to your message, which undermines storytelling and viewer comprehension.

### Rule: Cuts as Default
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Cuts are the default transition choice and the least obtrusive. They work best on action.
- **Reasoning:** Cuts feel natural and invisible to the brain. They don't add latency or feel precious. They're the industry standard for a reason: they don't get in the way.

### Rule: Dissolves Signal Transitions
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Dissolves signal time passage or a shift in topic. Keep them brief—don't let the overlap run longer than necessary.
- **Reasoning:** Dissolves create a visual pause that cues the audience to recalibrate. Extended dissolves feel indulgent and slow down pacing. Quick dissolves (0.5–1 second) feel intentional.

### Rule: Speed Ramps for Visual Interest
- **Applies to:** Montages, motion-heavy content
- **Rule:** Zoom in slowly on footage while moving to the next segment to add visual interest without distraction.
- **Reasoning:** Speed ramps are kinetic and feel dynamic without disrupting flow. They're subtle enough not to break immersion but visible enough to maintain energy.

### Rule: J-Cut and L-Cut Smoothness
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Overlap audio from the next clip while still showing the previous video (L-cut), or vice versa (J-cut), for smooth flow.
- **Reasoning:** Overlapping audio and video creates a bridge that makes transitions feel intentional and seamless. It prevents jarring silence or abrupt visual changes.

### Rule: Avoid Over-Used Transitions
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Over-using dissolves or flashy transitions distracts from content.
- **Reasoning:** Repetition becomes visible. If every transition is a spin or a wipe, the audience stops seeing the content and starts noticing the editing. Reserve special transitions for earned moments.

### Rule: No Arbitrary Surprises
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Transitions shouldn't feel arbitrary or surprising to the viewer.
- **Reasoning:** Predictability creates comfort. An unexpected transition jolts the viewer out of immersion. Transitions should feel inevitable in context.

### Rule: Professional Defaults
- **Applies to:** Professional, commercial content
- **Rule:** In professional work, most transitions should be cuts or subtle dissolves.
- **Reasoning:** Restraint signals competence. Over-decorated transitions read as amateur or low-budget. Professionals let content speak for itself.

### Rule: Transitions Bridge Ideas
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Use transitions to bridge ideas, not just because they look cool.
- **Reasoning:** Transitions have semantic meaning. They signal relationships between segments. Misusing them creates logical confusion.

### Rule: Music Stings Signal Change
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Transitions with music help signal to the viewer's brain that something is about to change.
- **Reasoning:** Audio is a powerful cue. A subtle music sting pairs with a visual transition to prepare the audience for a mental shift. This reduces cognitive friction.

### Rule: Short Music Stings
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Short music stings and transitions (1–5 seconds) feel professional.
- **Reasoning:** Extended stings feel indulgent and break pacing. Quick music cues maintain momentum while signaling intentionality.

### Rule: Visible Transitions for Hooks
- **Applies to:** Short-form video (reels, shorts, TikTok)
- **Rule:** Use visible transitions (wipeLeft, wipeRight) to signal the hook is different from the main content.
- **Reasoning:** In short-form content, the hook is a distinct narrative moment. A visible transition creates a mental reset that prepares the audience for the body.

## Tips

- **Let action guide your transitions.** Cut on a punch, a turn, a gesture. Action-based cuts feel natural because the eye is already moving.
- **Test transitions with the sound off.** If a transition works without audio cues, it's truly motivated. If it falls flat, it was probably decorative.
- **Use the J-cut when you want the next speaker or idea to feel like a natural follow-up.** Overlap their audio while visuals are still settling, and the transition feels inevitable.
- **Save flashy transitions for moments that earn them.** If you have one whip or spin in your entire video, place it on the most important moment—it'll feel earned and memorable.
- **Match transition speed to pacing.** Fast content needs quick cuts; slow, meditative content can afford longer dissolves. Transition timing should echo the overall rhythm of your piece.
