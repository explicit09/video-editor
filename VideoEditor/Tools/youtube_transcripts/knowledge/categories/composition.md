# Composition Knowledge

## Rules

### Rule: Draw Viewer Attention Intentionally
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Use framing, vignettes, power windows, and spatial tools to direct eye to important elements. Every compositional choice should serve a viewing purpose.
- **Reasoning:** Without intentional direction, viewers' eyes wander and miss key information. Deliberate composition guides comprehension and emotional focus.

### Rule: Vignetting for Subject Focus
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Subtle vignetting controls viewer attention and adds depth. It's particularly effective for isolating subjects in bland backgrounds without drawing attention to the technique itself.
- **Reasoning:** Vignetting creates spatial hierarchy that pulls focus inward. When applied subtly, it feels natural while effectively isolating the important element from distracting backgrounds.

### Rule: Organic Details Over Artificial Elements
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Film grain, dust, and hair elements must blend naturally into the image. Pixelated or glowing artifacts break immersion and feel amateurish.
- **Reasoning:** Viewers' minds detect artificial or incongruent details instinctively. Organic imperfections feel real; artificial ones feel like mistakes or poor technique.

### Rule: Wide Shot Establishes Context
- **Applies to:** Interviews and podcasts
- **Rule:** Wide shots establish both people and their surrounding space. Use wide shots at the beginning of sequences to orient viewers.
- **Reasoning:** Wide shots provide context and environmental storytelling. They answer "where are we?" before cutting to closer perspectives that answer "who is speaking?"

### Rule: Close-Ups Alternate for Engagement
- **Applies to:** Interviews
- **Rule:** Close-ups alternating between speakers provide variety and keep attention on faces. Don't stay in one framing for extended periods.
- **Reasoning:** Facial expression conveys emotion and emphasis. Alternating close-ups maintain visual interest and reinforce the conversational dynamic.

### Rule: Consistent Framing Across Angles
- **Applies to:** Multi-camera productions
- **Rule:** Maintain consistent framing across camera angles so cuts between them don't feel jarring. Similar headroom, looking room, and distance create smooth transitions.
- **Reasoning:** Inconsistent framing disorients viewers during cuts. Consistent framing makes angle changes imperceptible and maintains viewing comfort.

### Rule: Headroom Composition
- **Applies to:** All content
- **Rule:** Leave space above the head following rule of thirds—avoid headroom that's too generous (awkward) or too tight (claustrophobic). Find the balance that feels natural.
- **Reasoning:** Headroom is a subtle but critical compositional element. It signals professionalism and respect for the subject. Extreme headroom either way communicates carelessness.

### Rule: Looking Room and Eye Line Direction
- **Applies to:** Interviews
- **Rule:** If a person looks toward the camera-right side, have space in that direction. Maintain consistent eye lines across multi-camera cuts so looking direction remains logical.
- **Reasoning:** Looking room creates space for the viewer's eye to follow. Inconsistent eye lines across cuts confuse spatial understanding and feel discontinuous.

### Rule: Vertical Video Requires Tight Framing
- **Applies to:** Shorts and vertical platforms
- **Rule:** Vertical video framing is much tighter than horizontal. Side-to-side movement is less effective; place more emphasis on face and gestures.
- **Reasoning:** Vertical frame is narrow. Horizontal movement wastes space; facial expression and hand gestures scale better in the constrained format.

### Rule: Safe Title Area in Vertical Format
- **Applies to:** Shorts and vertical content
- **Rule:** Safe title area is smaller in vertical frames. Don't place text too close to edges or it will be cut off or obscured on mobile devices.
- **Reasoning:** Mobile safe areas are more constrained than desktop. Text placed near edges will be hidden by notches, rounded corners, and interface elements.

### Rule: Auto-Reframe Starting Point
- **Applies to:** Shorts conversion from 16:9
- **Rule:** Use auto-reframe tools to convert 16:9 to 9:16 vertical, but treat this as a starting point, not a final solution. Manually adjust for proper subject positioning.
- **Reasoning:** Auto-reframe can't anticipate compositional intent. Manual refinement ensures subjects remain properly framed and the conversion feels intentional rather than automated.

## Tips

1. **Use power windows strategically** - Create subtle power windows (masks) around secondary subjects to maintain focus on primary speakers. Keep the mask soft so viewers don't consciously see it.

2. **Match wide-to-close-up ratios** - Establish a rhythm for when you cut from wide to close-up. Most interviews work well with 60% close-ups and 40% wider angles, but adjust based on content.

3. **Measure headroom with the rule of thirds** - Divide the frame into thirds horizontally. Position eyes on the upper third line for balanced headroom that feels professional and intentional.

4. **Use depth of field as compositional tool** - Shallow depth of field (blurred background) isolates the subject naturally. Deep depth of field works when context is important. Choose based on whether background matters.

5. **Test vertical conversions on mobile** - When converting horizontal footage to vertical, always preview on an actual mobile device, not just your monitor. Screen size and viewing angle change perception significantly.
